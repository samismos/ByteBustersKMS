Motherboard

Why is it so important?

According to the computer's core printed circuit board definition, a motherboard is the central component of the circuitry that all other components plug into to form a functioning whole.
The motherboard serves as the backbone of the computer, connecting all of its parts and enabling communication between them. None of the components of the computer, including the hard disk, GPU, and CPU, could interact without it. For a computer to function properly, the motherboard must be fully functional. If your motherboard is malfunctioning, be prepared for some serious issues.

Motherboard or Transformers?

We understand the confusion, but an extremely complex system is required in order to provide a safe connection point for all other components to form the computer as we know it. We call it the nervous system because it receives the organs (components) and outputs a fully functional body (PC).

What are all these different shapes and holes?

Some of the basic ports and connectors the motherboard offers are the CPU socket, the RAM slots, the PCIe connectors, SATA ports, M.2 slots, input/output connectors and all the different power connectors. These are all utilised by specific components which form a cohesive system.

CPU socket

The CPU socket is a special square or parallelogram shaped connector in which...you guessed it, we place the CPU. It's important to purchase a motherboard model that comes with a CPU socket which is compatible with your CPU.

RAM slots

RAM sticks need a place to sleep, so think of these as their beds. Now keep in mind, your motherboard must support the same version of DDR as your memory. For example, DDR3 memory modules are not compatible with a DDR4 motherboard and vice versa.

SATA, M.2, PCIe

These are data communication protocols which are usually used by storage devices, such as hard drives and SSDs, as well as graphics cards and other interface cards (network, audio). There is no fear of incompatibility, however, if you want to get maximum performance out of your components, you should look for a motherboard which enables them to operate at 100%.

Are they all the same size?

No, motherboards come in different sizes. Typically, we'll see Mini-ATX, Micro-ATX, ATX and E-ATX. Usually average consumers prefer Micro-ATX and ATX cards because they fit easily in most cases and include plenty of features and capabilities.

It looks a little chaotic...

It is true that for first time builders, connecting every single wire on the motherboard may be the trickiest part. You are almost guaranteed to make a mistake, but as long as you adhere to the manual, you're unlikely to cause any damage. Have fun and learn!
